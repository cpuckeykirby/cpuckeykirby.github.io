Research Interests

Although I have a broad education in economics, I am particularly drawn to three key, interrelated areas: Development Economics, Macroeconomics, and Public Economics.

In Development Economics, I explore how structural policies, institutional reforms, and targeted interventions can spur growth and reduce inequality in emerging markets—a field that is especially appealing because of its direct impact on improving living standards and driving sustainable progress.

In Macroeconomics, I examine the broader dynamics of economic activity, including fiscal and monetary policy, consumption volatility, and financial market liberalization, with the goal of understanding how aggregate trends and shocks influence overall economic stability and growth. I combined these two interests in my Master's dissertation, focusing on how financial deregulation affects consumption volatility in developing countries.

Lastly, Public Economics captivates me through its focus on the design and evaluation of government policies—such as taxation and public expenditure—that aim to balance efficiency with social equity, thereby ensuring that economic progress benefits a broad spectrum of society. Together, these areas offer a robust framework for addressing critical economic challenges and shaping policies that foster both development and stability.

Master's Dissertation

Explore my Master's dissertation examining the impact of financial regulation liberalization on consumption volatility in developing countries.

The objective of this paper is to explore how the liberalisation of the financial sector affects private household consumption volatility in South Korea. We focus on both the liberalisation of the domestic sector and the foreign sector. Using data from 1973-2014, the results show that liberalisation of the domestic sector is more strongly associated with lower consumption volatility than liberalisation of the foreign sector. We compare these results to Ang (2011), who examines India, finding them contradictory. We conclude that the differences are a result of the different levels of financial development at the time of liberalisation.

How Does Financial Liberalisation Affect Consumption Volatility? Evidence from Korea
